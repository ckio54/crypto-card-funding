## How a Crypto Card Top-Up Actually Works

A crypto card top-up is, at its core, an on-chain transfer from a wallet or exchange into a deposit address controlled by the card issuer. Once the blockchain confirms the transfer, the issuer credits your card balance, usually in a fiat currency like HKD, USD, or EUR. From that point, the card behaves like a normal prepaid Visa — merchants receive local currency, and the crypto complexity stays in the background.

The part that trips people up is the middle layer: the coin-to-fiat conversion. Some issuers handle this automatically at settlement, embedding a spread into the exchange rate. Others charge an explicit conversion fee on top of the network fee. PokePay, for example, charges a 1% recharge fee on incoming crypto top-ups and a separate 1% spending fee on transactions, plus a 1% FX fee when the purchase currency differs from the card's base currency. That's three distinct fee layers, and understanding which one applies when is the difference between a predictable reload and a surprise decline.

## Step-by-Step: How to Top Up a Crypto Card with USDT

Using USDT as the example — because it's the most widely supported top-up asset across crypto cards, including PokePay — here's the practical flow. The interface varies by provider, but the logic is the same everywhere.

**Step 1: Log in and open the card management screen.** Inside the card app, look for a button labelled "Top Up," "Recharge," "Add Funds," or "Deposit." On PokePay, this lives in the card section after you've activated your PokeCard.

**Step 2: Copy the deposit address for the correct network.** This is the single most important step. The app will display a deposit address tied to a specific blockchain network — for USDT, that's typically TRC20, ERC20, or sometimes a native chain like C-Chain. Copy the address exactly as shown, and note which network it belongs to.

**Step 3: Send USDT from your wallet or exchange to that address.** In your exchange (OKX, Binance, etc.) or self-custodial wallet, initiate a withdrawal, paste the card's deposit address, and — critically — select the same network the card app specified. Sending TRC20 USDT to an ERC20 address, or vice versa, is the most common way a top-up fails.

**Step 4: Confirm and wait for blockchain settlement.** TRC20 transfers usually settle in under three minutes. ERC20 can take a few minutes depending on gas. BTC can take 10 to 40 minutes when the network is busy. Once confirmed, the card balance updates automatically.

**Step 5: Verify the balance before spending.** Check that the credited amount matches your send minus network fees. If you sent $100 USDT and only $99.XX shows up, that's the network fee on the sending side, not a hidden card charge — though the card's own recharge fee will also apply on the receiving side.

> **A habit worth keeping:** Send a small test transfer — say $3 to $5 — the first time you fund a card from a new wallet or a new network. Confirm it lands, then send the rest. The few cents of network fee on a test is the cheapest insurance in crypto.

## Common Top-Up Failures and How to Avoid Them

Most failed top-ups trace back to a small, repeatable set of mistakes. None of them are exotic, and all of them are preventable.

1. **Wrong network selection.** This is the number one cause of stuck or lost transfers. A USDT deposit address on TRC20 will not accept ERC20 USDT, and the reverse is equally true. Always match the network shown in the card app, not the one your wallet defaults to.
2. **Sending an unsupported coin.** If the card only lists USDT, USDC, BTC, and ETH, sending any other asset — even a popular one like SOL or MATIC — risks the funds being delayed or unrecoverable. Check the supported list every time you reload from a new wallet.
3. **Ignoring deposit limits.** Daily, per-transaction, and minimum top-up caps exist. PokePay's daily spending limit is HK$100,000 (around $12,500 USD), with a minimum single transaction of 0.1 HKD. Exceeding a cap can bounce a large reload.
4. **Skipping the address check.** A mistyped or phished address sends funds somewhere you cannot recover them from. Paste the address, then verify the first and last four characters against the card app before confirming.
5. **Insufficient gas on the sending side.** Your wallet may reject the withdrawal if you don't have enough native token (TRX for TRC20, ETH for ERC20) to cover network fees.

> **Quick rule:** If a top-up hasn't arrived within 30 minutes and the network shows the transfer as confirmed, contact the card issuer's support with the transaction hash. Most issues resolve within a day, but waiting longer only reduces your recovery options.

## Security Habits That Keep Your Top-Ups Safe

Funding a crypto card means moving real value across public blockchains, which makes a few security habits non-negotiable.

- **Enable two-factor authentication on your card account.** Funding starts with a secure login. If the card app offers 2FA, turn it on before your first reload.
- **Use the one-click card lock when you're not actively spending.** PokePay includes this feature — if you're topping up but not planning to transact immediately, locking the card prevents unauthorized charges while funds sit on the balance.
- **Keep CVV and expiry visible only inside the app.** Don't screenshot card details or store them in plain text. Multiple incorrect CVV entries can lock the card and require support intervention to unlock.
- **Verify the deposit address every time.** Even if you've funded the card before, re-check the address on a new session. Address poisoning attacks can replace a copied address with a lookalike.
- **Don't store large balances long-term.** A crypto card is a spending tool, not a savings account. Top up what you need for the near term and keep the rest in your exchange or self-custodial wallet.

## Frequently Asked Questions About Topping Up a Crypto Card

**Can I top up a crypto card with more than one cryptocurrency at the same time?**
Each top-up uses a single cryptocurrency on a single network. To fund with several assets, send separate transfers, each on its correct network. They'll all credit to the same card balance.

**Why did my USDT top-up fail or stay pending?**
The usual causes are: wrong network selection, sending an unsupported coin, insufficient gas fees on the sending side, exceeding deposit or regional limits, or an incomplete KYC status. Check the network match first — that solves the majority of failures.

**Are there fees to top up a crypto card?**
Yes, typically across three layers: the blockchain network fee (varies by chain), the issuer's top-up fee (PokePay charges 1%), and any conversion fee if the card converts crypto to fiat at settlement. Always review the fee breakdown inside the app before confirming a reload.

**How much should I top up at once?**
Enough to cover your near-term spending plus a small buffer, without leaving a large idle balance on the card. For routine use, a weekly reload that matches your expected spend is a sensible pattern. For a first test, send a small amount ($3–$5) to confirm the flow before committing larger funds.

**Can I withdraw funds back from the card to my wallet?**
On PokePay, yes — you can withdraw your balance back to an exchange wallet address, with only network fees applying and no extra withdrawal fee on the platform side. This is useful if you've over-funded the card or want to move value back to crypto holdings.
